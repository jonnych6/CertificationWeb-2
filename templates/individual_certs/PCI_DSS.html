Everything you need to know about Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard

What is PCI-DSS?

The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS) was established in 2004 by major credit card companies, including Visa, MasterCard, Discover Financial Services, JCB International, and American Express. Governed by the Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council (PCI SSC), this set of security standards aims to protect credit and debit card transactions from data theft and fraud.

Although the PCI SSC does not have legal authority to enforce compliance, adherence to PCI DSS is mandatory for any business that processes credit or debit card transactions. Achieving PCI certification is widely regarded as the best way to protect sensitive data and information, fostering long-lasting and trusting relationships between businesses and their customers.

What does PCI DSS do?

The PCI-DSS ensures the protection of cardholder data through stringent requirements for encryption, security policies, vulnerability management, and access control. It mandates robust network security measures, including firewalls and up-to-date systems. Organizations must regularly update software and apply security patches, implement access controls with unique IDs and physical security, and restrict access to authorized personnel only. Continuous monitoring and testing of security systems are essential. Additionally, firms must maintain a comprehensive, ongoing information security policy. The PCI-DSS integrates all these components to ensure the effective protection of cardholder data.

Why do companies need PCI DSS?

The Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS) is crucial for companies that process payment card transactions. These companies handle a significant amount of sensitive information, which must be securely protected. Compliance with PCI DSS is typically required to do business with card networks, banks, and other financial institutions. Failure to comply with PCI DSS can result in substantial fines.

What are the PCI compliance levels?

PCI compliance is categorized into four levels based on the annual number of credit or debit card transactions a business processes. The classification level dictates the specific requirements an enterprise must meet to maintain compliance.

Level Criteria Requirements
1 Merchants processing over six million real-world credit or debit card transactions annually. Annual internal audit conducted by an authorized PCI auditor.
Quarterly PCI scans by an Approved Scanning Vendor (ASV).
2 Merchants processing between one and six million real-world credit or debit card transactions annually. Annual assessment using a Self-Assessment Questionnaire (SAQ).
Quarterly PCI scans may be required.
3 Merchants processing between 20,000 and one million e-commerce transactions annually. Annual assessment using the relevant SAQ.
Quarterly PCI scans may be required.
4 Merchants processing fewer than 20,000 e-commerce transactions annually or up to one million real-world transactions. Annual assessment using the relevant SAQ.
Quarterly PCI scans may be required.

What are the 12 PCI DSS requirements?

The PCI SSC has established 12 requirements for handling cardholder data and maintaining a secure network, organized under six key goals. Compliance with all these requirements is essential for enterprises to achieve PCI DSS certification.

Requirement Examples
1 Secure Network Install and maintain a firewall configuration.
Use original system passwords (not vendor-supplied).
2 Protect Cardholder Data Ensure stored cardholder data is protected.
Encrypt cardholder data transmitted across public networks.
3 Vulnerability Management Use and regularly update anti-virus software.
Develop and maintain secure systems and applications.
4 Access Control Restrict access to cardholder data based on business needs.
Assign a unique ID to every individual with computer access.
Limit physical access to cardholder data.
5 Network Monitoring and Testing Track and monitor access to cardholder data and network resources.
Regularly test security systems and processes.
6 Information Security Maintain a comprehensive information security policy.
