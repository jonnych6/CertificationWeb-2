NIST

Everything you need to know about National Institute of Standards and Technology

What is NIST?

NIST is the National Institute of Standards and Technology at the U.S. Department of Commerce. The NIST Cybersecurity Framework helps businesses with setting standards for technology. NIST creates and manages standards related to technology, and data for promoting innovation and industrial competitiveness. Since its publication, the Framework for Improving Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity by NIST has been the most relevant document for the protection of information and it has become a necessity standard for many organizations that process a lot of sensitive information.

What is the Purpose of NIST?

The purpose of NIST is to provide a resource for the overall standards and guidelines that provide security for information systems. The standards allow for improved economic security in society while enhancing the quality of life through driving innovation and competitiveness by measurement science, standards, and technology. The framework from NIST allows organizations to manage and reduce cybersecurity risk. It also ensures federal organizations comply with the Federal Information Security Modernization Act.

Why Do Companies Need to Follow NIST?

Companies need to follow NIST for a number of important reasons. First, compliance with NIST is compulsory for companies that work with federal agencies. This requirement means ensuring that those contractors have good security measures put in place for the protection of sensitive government data. NIST provides a framework for managing cybersecurity risks. This is critical for making sure organizational data and systems are secure from cyber threats. Moreover, it indicates that the practices in the security measures taken to protect clients and their partners. Besides, compliance with NIST normally satisfies most of the requirements under other security standards, such as PCI DSS and SOX, this saves a lot of time and resources in the implementation of several standards of compliance.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

This framework is a guide and best practices for cyber security. It is divided into three tiers: core, profile, and tiers. It covers activities that identify, protect, detect, respond, and recover from incidents.

NIST Special Publication 800-53

This standard details the security controls that comply with FISMA and FIPS 200. It covers 18 areas from access control, incident response, disaster recovery. Also helps conduct risk assessment.

NIST Special Publication 800-171

Covers the organizations that have contracts with the U.S. Department of Defense and handle Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI). Comply with the Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS). It provides a guideline on how to protect the confidentiality of Controlled Unclassified Information.

Which NIST is Right for You?

The right NIST standard for you will depend on your organization's needs and the nature of your work:

  • NIST CSF: This framework is designed for organizations that want to improve their cybersecurity posture and collaborate with other frameworks, such as PCI DSS and SOX.
  • NIST 800-53: This is the most targeted publication towards federal agencies and government contractors looking for full-scale security controls and risk management.
  • NIST 800-171: This framework is DoD contractors-specific in handling CUI with DFARS compliance to ensure data protection under these contractors.

What is the purpose of NIST 800 53?

The purpose of NIST SP 800-53 is to enhance the security of information systems utilized by the federal government. The purpose of NIST is to furnish guidelines in the form of a catalog of controls, which facilitate the development of secure information systems.

The process takes you through categorizing your information system at a low-, medium-, or high-security level. With these categories, you compare the NIST 800-53’s security catalog and determine which applies to your firm.

Who needs to comply with NIST 800 53?

By mandate, US-based contractors and federal government agencies need to comply with NIST 800 53. However, other state and private organizations still use it as their security control framework.

If you’re unaware of the systems on your network or if you’re caught off-guard by unexpected admin accounts or abnormal applications, you lack a baseline from which to manage your network.

NIST 800 53 will serve as a bird’s eye view of your network, down to the tiniest details. This level of visibility is important.

What is NIST 800 171?

NIST 800 171 is a publication that describes the required security standards and practices for non-federal organizations that handle CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information) on their networks.

These guidelines must be complied with by any organization handling or storing sensitive, unclassified data on behalf of the US government.

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), which initially published it in June 2015, then released a number of standards and publications to improve cybersecurity resilience in both the public and private sectors. NIST 800 171 has undergone regular updates in response to emerging technological challenges and new cyber threats.

What is the purpose of NIST 800 171?

The purpose of NIST 800 171 is to establish guidelines for protecting sensitive data on the IT networks and systems used by federal contractors. By enforcing the best cybersecurity measures from government contractors, organizations are able to strengthen their federal supply chain.

The document stresses the significance of customizing security measures to correspond with a company’s unique goal, operational needs, risk tolerance, and operating environment. By doing this, NIST 800-170 hopes to guarantee that cybersecurity operations are optimized and pertinent and successfully handle the particular difficulties that each organization faces.

Who does NIST 800 171 apply to?

NIST 800 171 applies to any organization that processes or keeps sensitive, unclassified information on behalf of the US government and is expected to adhere to the National Institute of Requirements and Technology Special Publication 800-171 (NIST SP 800-171) cybersecurity requirements.

Departments of the US government depend on a variety of external institutions and service providers to run. Many of these crucial services include the processing and storage of sensitive data on the IT networks of contractors.

NIST 800 171 continues to expand the range of organizations that it applies to. NIST 800 171 is currently a contractual requirement for the information systems of any non-federal organization that manages, transmits, stores, or safeguards Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) for the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA), General Services Administration (GSA), or Department of Defence (DoD).

Here are some of these organizations:

  • Healthcare data processors
  • Defense contractors
  • Web and communication service providers
  • Organizations providing financial services
  • Research institutes and labs receiving federal grants and information
  • Systems Integrators
  • Colleges and universities that utilize federal data or information

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Non-compliance with the NIST standards can lead to severe penalties. For federal contractors, non-compliance can mean denial of contracts, especially for DoD contractors. An organization may also face financial fines and penalties for non-conformity to NIST standards. Moreover, non-compliance affects the organization's reputation since it leads to loss of trust from clients and stakeholders.
