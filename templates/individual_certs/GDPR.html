Everything you need to know about General Data Protection Regulation

What is GDPR?

The GDPR is the result of the EU's comprehensive data protection reform. The strict privacy standards went into effect on May 25, 2018, to protect individuals' rights. This cybersecurity framework aims to protect all people's personal data in the European Union and complements other EU regulations such as the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA).

The GDPR modernizes the 1950 European Convention on Human Rights for the digital era. Article 8 of the convention guarantees everyone's right to privacy in family life. The distinction between public and private life was clear in the analog era, but it is often unclear now. Customers cannot be confident that their private data, and thus their private lives, are being respected in the absence of a clear and enforced standard such as the GDPR.

Who Does the GDPR Apply To?

The GDPR applies to any organization that provides goods and services to people in the EU. This includes entities not based in the EU. If you run an online business, you never know for sure whether the customers you deal with are in the EU. As a result, all online businesses should become GDPR-compliant as a precaution. Personal data is divided into two categories: controllers and processors.

Data Controllers

According to the GDPR, a controller is any individual, public authority, agency, or other body that determines the purpose and processing of personal data. Controllers determine how personal data is processed. For example, a music school uses a digital screen to alert parents in the waiting room when each teacher is ready. The screen shows each child's name and the room number for their music lesson. The music school is designated as the "controller" of personal data because it determines how the notification system will process all of the data.

Data Processors

The GDPR defines processors as any individual or entity that processes personal data on behalf of a controller, without making decisions on data handling. For example, if a software company (the controller) hires a marketer (the processor) to send personalized emails using provided data, the software company sets the rules, and the marketer follows them. Despite following instructions, processors must still comply with GDPR since they handle personal data.

8 Key regulations of GDPR

# Regulation Point Details
1 Data Protection Principles Process data lawfully, fairly, and transparently; for specified purposes; with data minimization; ensuring accuracy; limiting storage; maintaining security; and demonstrating compliance.
2 Accountability Demonstrate compliance through detailed documentation, designated responsibilities, staff training, and contracts with data processors.
3 Data Security Implement technical measures (e.g., encryption) and organizational measures (e.g., staff training) to protect data. Notify data subjects of breaches within 72 hours unless data is safeguarded.
4 Data Protection by Design and Default Integrate data protection principles into the design of new products and processes.
5 Lawful Data Processing Process data only with consent, for contractual obligations, legal requirements, vital interests, public tasks, or legitimate interests, with proper documentation and transparency.
6 Consent Must be clear, specific, informed, and revocable. Consent for children under 13 requires parental permission.
7 Data Protection Officers (DPOs) Required for public authorities, large-scale monitoring, or processing of sensitive data. Optional but beneficial for all organizations.
8 Privacy Rights Individuals have rights to be informed, access, rectification, erasure, restriction, data portability, objection, and protection from automated decisions.

If you process data, you must adhere to the seven protection and accountability principles outlined in Article 5.1-2:

Principle Details
1 Lawfulness, Fairness, and Transparency Processing must be lawful, fair, and transparent to the data subject.
2 Purpose Limitation Data must be processed only for the legitimate purposes specified explicitly to the data subject when collected.
3 Data Minimization Collect and process only the data that is absolutely necessary for the specified purposes.
4 Accuracy Personal data must be accurate and kept up to date.
5 Storage Limitation Personally identifying data may only be stored for as long as necessary to fulfill the specified purpose.
6 Integrity and Confidentiality Processing must ensure appropriate security, integrity, and confidentiality, such as through encryption.
7 Accountability The data controller is responsible for demonstrating compliance with all of these principles.

Fines and Penalties of GDPR

The GDPR imposes significant fines to ensure data protection compliance for businesses of all sizes. Under Article 83, fines are categorized into two tiers based on the severity of the infringement:

  • Less Severe Violations

    Fines can reach up to €10 million or 2% of the company’s global annual revenue, whichever is higher. This includes violations related to data controllers and processors, certification bodies, and monitoring bodies.

  • More Severe Violations

    Fines can be as high as €20 million or 4% of the company’s global annual revenue, whichever is greater. This includes breaches of core GDPR principles, conditions for consent, data subjects' rights, and international data transfers. Additional fines may be imposed for violations of national laws or non-compliance with supervisory authority orders.

Determining GDPR Fines

Fines are assessed by data protection regulators in each EU country using a range of criteria, including the gravity and nature of the violation, intention, mitigation efforts, and history of compliance. Regulators also consider factors such as data type, cooperation, and any aggravating or mitigating circumstances.

Responsibility for Data Controllers

Companies remain fully responsible for GDPR compliance even when using third-party services. They must ensure that third parties adhere to GDPR standards and can be held liable for any non-compliance.
