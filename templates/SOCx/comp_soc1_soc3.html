SOC 1 vs. SOC 3

Overview of SOC Reports

SOC 1 and SOC 3 reports are commonly requested by buyers in North America to evaluate different aspects of their service providers' controls. SOC 1 reports focus on internal controls related to financial reporting. Buyers use these reports to assess how a service organization’s controls impact their financial statements and compliance with accounting standards.

On the other hand, SOC 3 reports provide a summary of a service organization’s controls related to security, availability, and confidentiality. These reports are often used for public assurance and marketing purposes, offering a high-level overview that is suitable for a broader audience.
Each type of SOC report serves a distinct purpose, helping buyers ensure their service providers meet specific standards for financial integrity and data protection.

What’s the difference between a SOC 1 and a SOC 3?

The key differences between SOC 1 and SOC 3 reports lie in their focus and intended use. Here’s a breakdown of each:

  • SOC 1 Report: This report details the controls your organization has in place related to financial reporting. It is primarily used by organizations whose services impact their clients' financial statements. SOC 1 reports provide assurance that the controls are designed and operating effectively to support accurate financial reporting.
  • SOC 3 Report: This report provides a summary of the controls related to security, availability, and confidentiality. SOC 3 reports are designed for public distribution and are often used for marketing purposes. They offer a high-level overview of a service organization’s controls and are intended for a broader audience who need assurance on the effectiveness of controls without the detailed specifics found in SOC 2 reports.
SOC 1 reports are primarily used to assess financial reporting controls, whereas SOC 3 reports offer a public assurance of a service organization’s controls over security, availability, and confidentiality.

Do you need a SOC 1 or SOC 3 report?

  • SOC 1 Report: If your services impact your clients' financial operations, a SOC 1 report is likely required. For example, organizations that process payments or handle financial transactions on behalf of their clients will need a SOC 1 report to ensure accurate financial reporting and demonstrate effective controls related to financial transactions.
  • SOC 3 Report: If you want to provide a high-level assurance of your controls related to security, availability, and confidentiality to a broad audience, a SOC 3 report is appropriate. This type of report is often used for marketing purposes and public assurance, offering a summary of your controls without the detailed specifics found in SOC 2 reports.
Your clients or stakeholders will determine the type of SOC report they require. However, it is recommended not to wait for these requests. Plan ahead for a SOC audit to minimize time and effort. Consider your organization’s needs and objectives to anticipate the type of SOC report that will best serve your clients and support your business goals.
